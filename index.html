Your Waterfowl Neighbors


It’s a little odd but quite common around the world that people like feeding ducks bread. We don’t know when people started to feed ducks bread. It just seems like people have done that for generations. I remember when I was a child, I often brought a bag of bread to the park, tore bite size pieces off, and threw them into water. Then a paddling of ducks would come and enjoyed the bread.

Then I came to University of Miami, finding the campus full of ducks. There’re definitely people feeding those ducks constantly, because if you pass by a team of ducks in the morning, then stand still in front of them, they will come to gather around you, like they believe you have food for them. I sometimes see parents bring their kids here to feed the ducks by the pond. Most duck food is still bread.

A survey in UK conducted in 2015* found in England and Wales alone, park visitors feed wild ducks an estimated 6 million loaves of bread every year, which is the weight equivalent of sixty Boeing 737.

While this is such a normal and traditional practice, I often wonder if any people ever feel somewhat suspicious and ask themselves“Is bread really for the ducks?” before they offer it to ducks.

With the help of Google trends data, we noticed that, besides bread, the most typically recognized waterfowl food, lots of people try to toss different kind of food to ducks, and they search in Google to see what food they can feed to ducks.

Search interest in feeding waterfowl vs bird migration

Google search interest in feeding waterfowl grows over years but still follows a consistent seasonal pattern. People are more willing to feed ducks and geese in early summer.

We all know that these migratory birds fly south, winter in mild areas where food is plentiful and the water rarely freezes like the Mississippi Alluvial Valley or coastal northern California. Then they migrate long distances from wintering grounds to northern nesting and breeding areas in spring.

Search interest in waterfowl hunting vs actual hunting season

A seasonal pattern can also be identified from people’s search on duck and goose hunting. This pattern reveals a strong correlation between Google search interest and waterfowl hunting season.

Searches for goose hunting share a similar trend with actual goose hunting season. Canada geese season usually starts in September and snow geese during January every year.

Meanwhile people’s interest in duck hunting always reaches a peak during December, around when the statewide duck season begins. Thanksgiving weekend marks the start of Waterfowl hunting season in Mississippi.

regional Search interest in waterfowl vs how waterfowl travels

The Mississippi region shows a significant search interest in duck hunting than others. The Mississippi River Alluvial Plain is the top destination for duck hunters. This is a major wintering area for millions of ducks and geese, which are of interest to hunters.

While the Midwest has more goose fans. The plains of Iowa, Minnesota, and the Dakotas are the start of the “Duck Factory”, Prairie Pothole Region. It supports globally significant populations of breeding waterfowl, nearly 3 in 4 North American ducks are raised here. Countless of ducks and geese pass through the PPR each spring, nesting in the grasslands.

Waterfowl Migration Flyways in North America

Migratory birds follow different routes as they migrate between nesting and wintering areas. Based on those routes, North America is divided into four flyways—the Atlantic, Mississippi, Central, and Pacific.

  • Pacific
  • Central
  • Mississippi
  • Atlantic

  • The Atlantic Flyway is a bird migration route that generally follows the Atlantic Coast of North America and the Appalachian Mountains.
  • The Central Flyway follows the Great Plains. Its council is composed of representatives in 10 states, two Canadian provinces and the Northwest Territories.
  • The Mississippi Flyway follows the Mississippi River. Good sources of water, food, and cover exist over its entire length. About 40% of all North American migrating waterfowl and shorebirds use this route.
  • The Pacific Flyway is a major north-south route of travel for migratory birds in America, extending from Alaska to Patagonia.
